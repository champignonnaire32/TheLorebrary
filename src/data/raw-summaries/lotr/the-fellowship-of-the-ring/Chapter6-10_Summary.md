### Book I - Chapter 6: The Old Forest
Merry wakes the hobbits early to depart Crickhollow before the thick mist clears. Fredegar Bolger stays behind while the other four ride sturdy ponies toward the Hedge. They enter the Old Forest through a locked iron gate in a brick-walled tunnel. The trees within the forest shift and move to block the hobbits' intended path north. They eventually reach the Bonfire Glade where hobbits once burned trees to defend the Hedge.

Heat and a heavy atmosphere oppress the group as they climb a treeless green hill. From the summit, they see the Withywindle valley but cannot find the East Road. The forest forces them to move right and downward into the heart of the valley. They follow a brook through a deep gully until they reach the slow-moving Withywindle river. A massive willow tree, Old Man Willow, casts a powerful sleep spell over the party.

Frodo falls into the water while Merry and Pippin are swallowed by cracks in the willow's trunk. Sam rescues Frodo from the river and tries to use fire to force the tree open. The willow tree responds by squeezing the trapped hobbits harder and causing them to scream. Tom Bombadil, a man in a blue coat and yellow boots, arrives singing nonsense songs. The hobbits enter Tom Bombadil's house and meet Goldberry, the River-daughter who wears a green gown and a gold belt. Tom provides the group with a feast of bread, butter, honey, and yellow cream. Frodo dreams of a man standing on a stone tower who is carried away by an eagle. The hobbits spend the next day indoors while a heavy rain falls over the forest. Tom tells stories about the ancient history of the forest and its malevolent trees.

After a second feast, Tom asks to see the Ring and places it on his finger. The Ring does not make Tom invisible, and he sees Frodo when Frodo puts it on. Tom returns the Ring to Frodo and warns him to stay on the green grass.

He teaches the hobbits a specific rhyme to call for his help if they find trouble. Goldberry sings songs about the rain and the cycle of the river water. Tom predicts the weather will be clear for their departure the following morning. The group retires to their beds with renewed confidence for the journey ahead. ### Book I - Chapter 8: Fog on the Barrow-Downs
The hobbits leave Tom's house and ride toward the northern edge of the Downs. They stop for a midday meal on a flat-topped hill near a tall standing stone. A sudden white fog surrounds the hill and traps the group in a dense mist. Frodo becomes separated from the others after passing between two huge standing stones. A Barrow-wight, a cold shadow with glowing eyes, captures Frodo on a hilltop.

Frodo wakes up inside a stone barrow alongside Sam, Merry, and Pippin. The other three hobbits are dressed in white rags and crowned with golden circlets. A long, pale arm crawls toward the hobbits to deliver a final killing blow. Frodo seizes a short sword and hacks off the Wight's hand before his blade shatters. He sings the rhyme Tom taught him, causing Tom Bombadil to arrive and break the barrow.

Tom drives the Wight away with a song and carries the treasure out of the mound. The hobbits wake up and find their ponies have returned with Tom's horse, Fatty Lumpkin. Tom gives each hobbit a dagger forged by the Men of Westernesse many years ago. He escorts the group to the East Road and points them toward the village of Bree. Tom refuses to cross his borders and returns home as the hobbits reach the Prancing Pony.
