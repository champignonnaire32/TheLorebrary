### Chapter 31: Tartarus
Hadrian is held for over a week in a concrete chamber alongside elderly nobles awaiting audiences with the Undying. The prisoners are fed at irregular intervals by twin serving girls in translucent gowns. To maintain his sanity, Hadrian reads historical texts on his terminal and uses his coat as a tent to hide from the constant yellow light. An elegant android named Yume enters the room, featuring clockwork visible through crystal panels and a single black eye with a golden tear filigree. Yume selects Lady Catherine for an audience but refuses Hadrian's request to send a message to his companions.

Hadrian spends another week reading and contemplating his visions of the Cielcin host and a black ship. He speaks with an elderly Countess who explains that the nobles are in Vorgossos to obtain "second lives." She reveals that Kharn Sagara grows clones, referred to as "children," to serve as new bodies for the wealthy. Hadrian is disgusted by this practice, recognizing it as cloning, which is one of the Chantry’s Twelve Abominations. He compares the desperate hunger for life in the eyes of the nobles to the painting of Saturn devouring his own son.

Baron Song joins the conversation and invites Hadrian to another game of Druaja. Hadrian realizes that these wealthy individuals are fully aware of the depraved nature of their quest for immortality. The atmosphere of the room feels increasingly funereal and charged with a sense of moral rot. Hadrian struggles to maintain a polite mask while his mind reels from the revelation of the cloning operation. He remains trapped in the waiting area, surrounded by those willing to consume children to escape death.



### Chapter 32: Saturn or Dis
Yume arrives to take Hadrian through a labyrinth of stone corridors and past many sealed metal doors. They board an open monorail cart that travels across a five-mile-deep abyss over a subterranean sea. The tram reaches a massive white stone pyramid where Hadrian is directed to enter the throne room alone. Inside, Kharn Sagara sits on a throne, his ageless face framed by wild hair and his bare chest wired with hoses and sensor tape. Sagara remains silent as Hadrian introduces himself and explains his mission to negotiate peace with the Cielcin.

A swarm of blue-eyed, leaf-shaped drones descends from the ceiling to project Sagara's voice throughout the chamber. Sagara mocks the "ilk" of people who come to him for new lives and quotes religious texts about the tree of knowledge. He examines Hadrian’s Jaddian highmatter sword, a gift from a Maeskolos, and offers to buy it for his collection. Hadrian refuses the offer and insists that the four-hundred-year war with the Cielcin must be ended to save lives. Sagara argues that war is as inevitable as gravity and expresses total indifference to the survival of the Empire.

Hadrian challenges the Undying by asking if he values any life other than his own. Sagara eventually agrees to hear the case of the Cielcin prisoner, Tanaran, and returns Hadrian's sword pommel-first. Yume reappears from the shadows to escort Hadrian back to the upper levels of the palace. Hadrian leaves the pyramid feeling the crushing weight of time and the inhumanity of the man who has lived for millennia.



### Chapter 33: Divide and Conquer
Hadrian is reunited with his companions in a richly appointed suite that has been cleared of previous occupants. Valka informs him that he was missing for twenty-three days while the rest of the group was kept together. Hadrian explains that Sagara has agreed to speak with Tanaran but requires the Cielcin to be brought from the ship. Yume informs the group that no electronic communications are allowed and that the orbital lift takes fifteen hours to ascend. Fearing that leaving together would look like a retreat, Hadrian decides to stay behind as a guest to demonstrate good faith.

Valka, Switch, and the others prepare to return to the Mistral to retrieve the Cielcin emissary. Switch expresses deep religious horror, calling the android a "daimon" and questioning if Hadrian is a replicate. The group walks through a lava tube tunnel to a tramway guarded by massive artillery and white stone pillars. Hadrian jokes with his friends about his middle name, Anaxander, to lighten the somber mood of their departure. Switch embraces Hadrian one last time, warning him that no one should be in such a house of horrors.

Valka lingers on the platform, pretending to adjust her boots after the others have boarded the tram. She surprises Hadrian with an embrace and tells him she is glad he is safe before retreating to the car. Hadrian watches the monorail disappear into the distance, leaving him alone with the android in the cold tunnel. He returns to the palace with Yume, feeling a renewed sense of isolation as his friends depart for the ship.



### Chapter 34: In the House of Kharn Sagara
Hadrian returns to his suite, where Yume provides him with a meal and instructions on using the laundry units. The android informs him that he is free to explore the installation but warns that Sagara will only speak in his own time. Hadrian wanders through the concrete corridors, which feel ancient and human compared to the alien ruins he has visited. He discovers a museum filled with artifacts, including Imperial Martian Guard armor and the shattered White Sword of a pretender. Following a side passage, he finds "The Orchid Stair," a series of round terraces filled with genetically engineered plants and animals.

The terraces are illuminated by imitation sunlight and house hummingbirds, squirrels, and tiny drones acting as bees. At the bottom of the garden, Hadrian finds a metal door depicting a man being devoured by a double helix of snakes. He attempts to open the door but finds it requires a holograph authentication that he does not possess. Yume appears and explains that these gardens are maintained for the "children" who serve as clone hosts. Hadrian is disturbed by the implication that the children live feral lives in the gardens before their inevitable deaths.

He is led back to his chambers, noticing how the android avoids direct answers about the cloning process. Once alone, Hadrian listens to a historical biography of the first emperors on his terminal while he eats. He studies a replica painting of "Cottages at Cordeville," finding comfort in the pre-Advent art from Old Earth. The painting allows him to briefly escape his anxieties about the Cielcin and the dead companions he left behind. As he reflects on his sketches in his journal, a pair of arms suddenly closes around his neck from behind.



### Chapter 35: The Gorgon
Hadrian reacts to the surprise attack by seizing the intruder and preparing to strike before realizing it is a woman. The woman, Naia, is a homunculus bed servant with large blue eyes and raven hair, sent by the Master. She wears a holograph dress that she dissolves to reveal her naked body, gold chains, and a snake anklet. Naia attempts to seduce Hadrian, but he remains on guard and circles the table to keep distance between them. He notices that Naia has a metallic spur implant behind her right ear, similar to those seen on Sagara.

Hadrian realizes that Naia is a SOM, or Slave of Mind, serving as a direct puppet for Kharn Sagara. Sagara begins speaking through Naia’s mouth, mocking Hadrian’s reaction and questioning his interest in Valka. The Undying admits that the real Naia is currently "sleeping" while he uses her body to interact with his guest. Hadrian is repulsed by the violation of the woman’s autonomy and demands that Sagara leave the room. Sagara uses Naia's hand to trace her own body in an obscene gesture, further taunting Hadrian's moral principles.

They argue about the nature of fear and art, with Sagara critiquing Hadrian’s black-and-white sketches. Sagara notes that the artist of the "Cottages at Cordeville" painting was considered too intense and frightening. Hadrian counters by suggesting that Sagara’s gray stone palace is a manifestation of his own deep-seated fears. The Undying laughs at the comparison and eventually agrees to take his leave of the suite. Sagara offers to leave the girl behind for Hadrian's amusement, but Hadrian repeats his demand for him to get out.
