### 11: At What Cost
Hadrian decides to escape and identifies two problems: getting offworld and paying for it. He realizes that as a palatine, he has access to accounts of imperial marks. He approaches Lena Balem, the Guild factionarius of the Delian Miners' Guild, offering a 120,000 mark donation. He uses this as a front to launder money, asking for a parallel contract of 130,000 marks while only filing for 150,000. He receives a universal card with 20,000 marks as his escape fund.

Hadrian feels shame for his deception but justifies the theft as necessary for his freedom. He returns to the penthouse in Meidua rather than the fortress of Devil's Rest. He is guarded by Lieutenant Kyra and two other hoplites. He reflects on his ability to slip away from guards, having done so during his previous mugging. He watches advertisements for the Meidua Devils gladiators and the Imperial Legions.

At the penthouse, Hadrian attempts to connect with Kyra, calling her by her first name. Kyra questions his familiarity, stating it is not proper for a servant and a palatine. Hadrian impulsively kisses her, but she freezes in shock and fear. Realizing she cannot refuse him due to his status, she offers to join him in bed. Hadrian, feeling sick and cowardly for the power imbalance, shouts a refusal and flees to his suite.



### 12: The Ugliness of the World
Hadrian realizes that any legitimate travel offworld would require a blood scan, exposing his identity. He avoids Kyra to escape the shame of their previous encounter and the power dynamic between them. Tor Alma performs medical enhancements on Hadrian to protect him against space radiation and offworld pathogens. Sir Felix concludes Hadrian's martial training a month before his scheduled departure. Hadrian spends his time walking with Gibson, discussing the dangers of hiring pirates for escape.

Gibson and Hadrian walk through the topiary gardens and onto the massive seawall of Devil's Rest. They cycle through multiple languages, including Jaddian and Lothrian, to avoid being understood by listeners or cameras. Gibson tells Hadrian that he has written a letter of recommendation for the scholiasts' athenaeum. Hadrian is stunned when Gibson admits they will never meet again due to the vastness of the Empire. The old scholiast emphasizes that despite the world's ugliness, there is peace and beauty in nature.

Gibson gives Hadrian a small leather-bound adventure novel titled The King with Ten Thousand Eyes by Kharn Sagara. Tucked inside the binding is the secret letter to the scholiasts that Hadrian needs for his escape. Gibson warns Hadrian that the ugliness of the world will come from all sides but urges him to focus on beauty. Hadrian hugs Gibson, realizing the old man is more of a father to him than Alistair Marlowe. They stand on the battlements as a shadow falls over Gibson, marking a final moment of peace.



### 13: The Scourging at the Pillar
Bells toll throughout Devil's Rest, signaling a special assembly before Hadrian's departure for the Chantry. Hadrian hides the novel containing Gibson's letter in his footlocker before joining the crowd in the plaza. Archon Alistair Marlowe appears on the balcony with Crispin and his lictors to address the castle staff. He announces that a traitor has been uncovered who planned to kidnap Hadrian and sell him to Extrasolarians. A whipping post is erected in the center of the plaza, and the crowd grows restless with anticipation.

Four hoplites drag Gibson, now dressed in green scholiast robes, to the whipping post. Alistair accuses Gibson of colluding with barbarians to steal his son, a charge Gibson falsely confesses to. Hadrian realizes Gibson is taking the blame for Hadrian's own escape plan to protect him from scrutiny. Sir Roban plays a doctored recording of Gibson's voice to confirm the conspiracy to the suspicious crowd. Alistair sentences Gibson to banishment from Delos and public scourging for his supposed crimes.

Sir Felix and a Chantry cathar approach Gibson to carry out the sentence. The cathar uses a plasma knife to slice Gibson's nostril, marking him as a criminal for life. Sir Felix then delivers fifteen lashes to Gibson's bare back, tearing his flesh and reducing him to cries of pain. Alistair strikes Hadrian in the face with a ringed fist when the boy tries to intervene. After being locked in his room, Hadrian discovers that his footlocker has been searched and the book containing the letter is gone.



### 14: Fear Is a Poison
Hadrian remains in his chambers for three days, paralyzed by the fear that his father's agents have discovered the letter. He realizes that without Gibson's recommendation, he will be forced to join the Chantry and become a torturer. He reflects on the hypocrisy of the Chantry, which uses the technologies it claims to hunt down to maintain power. On the day of his departure, a suborbital shuttle arrives to take Hadrian and Crispin to the summer palace in Haspida. The weather is stormy as Hadrian prepares to see his mother for the last time.

Crispin joins Hadrian at the airfield, expressing excitement about visiting the summer palace and the prospect of war. Hadrian feels a deep desolation, comparing his current state to a prisoner awaiting execution. They discuss the Cielcin, the only other spacefaring race, and Hadrian expresses a wish for peace that Crispin labels heresy. Crispin reveals that Alistair is seeking a barony in the Veil and intends for the family to move offworld eventually. Hadrian ignores his brother's attempts at conversation, feeling a growing distance between them.

The brothers board the shuttle as lightning flashes and the bells of Devil's Rest chime the hour. Hadrian tells Crispin that he refuses to serve the Chantry, sparking an argument about faith and the history of Earth. He dismisses the religious significance of Earth, calling it merely a rock that does not answer prayers. As the shuttle accelerates and leaves the ground, the massive carillon in the fortress rings a thirteenth time. Hadrian feels his heart vanish into the storm as he leaves his home behind forever.



### 15: The Summer Palace
Hadrian and Crispin arrive at the summer palace in Haspida, but their mother is not there to greet them. Hadrian spends his time drawing in his sketchbook, trying to capture the likeness of Gibson from memory. He reflects on the fallibility of memory and how the scholiasts are said to never forget anything. The water gardens outside his window are filled with dying lilies and serving girls bathing in the pools. Hadrian feels a sense of melodrama in his grief, recalling Gibson's disdain for the art form.

Crispin enters Hadrian's room and reveals that their mother has gone to Euclid for unknown reasons. He describes the vicereine's harem, which includes thirty-seven concubines and a blue-skinned homunculus. Crispin offers to take Hadrian to the harem to meet the "blue girl" or one of the boys as a parting experience. Hadrian refuses, realizing that his brother does not actually know him despite their shared genetics. He views his palatine family as a cabal of strangers bound only by blood and political convenience.

Several days pass without word from their mother, and Hadrian sinks into a cycle of sleeping to escape his anxiety. He recognizes that as a palatine, he is a pawn of the state, lacking the freedom of a common plebeian. During a morning run around the palace fortifications, a servant informs him that his mother has finally returned. The servant insists that the meeting is urgent and that Hadrian must go directly to her studio. Hadrian follows the man to a detached villa built in the Pre-Peregrine style to meet with the vicereine.
