### 1: MINAS TIRITH
Gandalf and Pippin arrive at Minas Tirith after a long ride on Shadowfax to warn Denethor of the coming war. They pass through the seven circles of the city, which is built into the side of Mount Mindolluin and features high white walls and a central citadel. Pippin meets Denethor, the Steward of Gondor, who is grieving the death of his elder son Boromir and holds the shards of the great horn. To honor Boromir's sacrifice, Pippin swears fealty to Denethor and is accepted into the service of the Lord of the City. The city prepares for siege as the armies of the southern fiefs arrive, including Forlong the Fat and Prince Imrahil of Dol Amroth.

Pippin is assigned to the Third Company of the Guard and befriends Beregond, a man-at-arms who teaches him the city's customs. He also meets Bergil, Beregond's son, who shows him the city and introduces him to the other boys remaining in the fortress. The sun is obscured by a vast cloud of smoke and ash flowing from Mordor, turning the day into a deep, brown twilight. Denethor questions Pippin extensively about Boromir's death and the nature of the quest, while Gandalf watches with growing concern. The Nazgûl, riding flying creatures resembling giant carrion-fowl, begin to circle high above the city and utter heart-quelling cries.

Pippin and Beregond watch the road from the battlements as the last refugees are sent away to the southern vales. A great shadow rises in the East, and Gandalf explains that it is a device of Sauron to spread despair and confusion among the defenders. Pippin feels the weight of the war and his smallness as a pawn in the great strategy directed by the Dark Lord. The chapter ends with the total disappearance of the dawn, as the Darkness begins to settle over the realm of Gondor. Pippin goes to sleep in his new quarters, listening for Gandalf's return while the wizard studies ancient scrolls by candlelight.




### 2: THE PASSING OF THE GREY COMPANY
Aragorn, Legolas, and Gimli are joined by Halbarad and thirty Dúnedain Rangers who have ridden from the North to support their kin. The sons of Elrond, Elladan and Elrohir, bring a message from their father advising Aragorn to remember the Paths of the Dead. Aragorn takes the Palantír of Orthanc and reveals himself to Sauron, showing the reforged sword Andúril to challenge the Dark Lord's will. This confrontation exhausts Aragorn but reveals a southern threat from the Corsairs of Umbar that will soon assail Minas Tirith. Aragorn decides to take the forbidden road through the Haunted Mountain to summon the Dead Men of Dunharrow to fulfill their ancient oath.

The Lady Éowyn pleads with Aragorn to remain or let her ride with him, but he refuses her request to seek glory in battle. The Grey Company enters the Dimholt, a dark valley at the root of the mountain, where a single black stone marks the entrance to the caves. Inside the lightless tunnels, they find the skeleton of Baldor, a prince of Rohan who died clawing at a locked stone door. Aragorn summons the Shadow-host, the spirits of men who broke their oath to Isildur, to follow him to the Stone of Erech. The Dead follow the company as a silent, pale army, causing terror among the living inhabitants of the Morthond Vale as they pass.

The company reaches the Hill of Erech at midnight, where a great black globe of stone from Númenor stands in the darkness. Aragorn blows a silver horn, and the King of the Dead answers from the night, agreeing to fight the servants of Sauron for peace. Halbarad unfurls a black standard wrought by Arwen, though its symbols remain hidden in the gloom as the company camps. At dawn, they begin a desperate and weary ride toward Pelargir, pursued by the ever-growing host of the Sleepless Dead. The group disappears into the thickening Storm of Mordor, leaving the abandoned villages of Lamedon behind as they race toward the sea.




### 3: THE MUSTER OF ROHAN
King Théoden and Merry arrive at the mountain stronghold of Dunharrow, where the people of Rohan have sought refuge from the coming war. Merry is appointed as the king's esquire and receives a small helm and a round shield bearing the device of the white horse. Éowyn informs the king that Aragorn has already departed into the Paths of the Dead, causing great dismay and fear among the Riders. An errand-rider named Hirgon arrives from Gondor and presents the Red Arrow, a traditional summons for immediate military aid to Minas Tirith. Théoden accepts the summons and vows to lead six thousand spears to the defense of the City, despite the heavy losses sustained at Isengard.

The muster of the Mark begins under a sky that has turned a permanent, featureless brown due to the smoke rising from the East. Théoden releases Merry from his service to stay with Éowyn, but the hobbit remains determined to follow his lord into the great battle. The host of the Rohirrim departs from Dunharrow and rides to Edoras, passing through villages where women watch from dark doorways in silence. A young Rider named Dernhelm approaches Merry and offers to carry him secretly to Gondor beneath his grey cloak on the horse Windfola. Merry accepts the offer and hides among the companies of Riders as they begin their long journey across the plains of Anórien.

The army camps in the willow-thickets of the Entwash, avoiding the main roads to evade the roving scouts of the Enemy. Rumors reach the king of orc-hosts attacking the northern borders, but Éomer insists they must press on toward the primary threat at Minas Tirith. The Riders pass the line of ancient beacon hills, but all the fires are quenched and the land is filled with a deepening gloom. Merry feels the weight of his smallness and the immensity of the task as he rides in silence through the sunless days. The chapter ends with the host approaching the borders of Gondor, moving as a silent shadow toward the fires of the besieged city.




### 4: THE SIEGE OF GONDOR
Pippin is dressed in the black and silver livery of the Tower Guard and begins his duties as an esquire to Denethor. The great shadow from Mordor completely covers the sky, and the Nazgûl on their winged steeds begin to dive and scream over the city. Faramir returns from Osgiliath under heavy attack and is rescued from the Black Riders by Gandalf, who wields a shaft of white light. In a tense council, Denethor rebukes Faramir for showing mercy to Frodo and expresses his wish that Boromir had survived in his stead. Faramir is sent back to lead a desperate defense of the outer walls at the River Anduin, despite the overwhelming numbers of the enemy.

The forces of Mordor breach the Rammas Echor and swarm over the Pelennor Fields, driving the defenders back toward the City gates. Faramir is struck by a poisoned dart during the retreat and is carried back to the Citadel unconscious and near death. Denethor, overwhelmed by grief and despair after looking into his own Palantír, abandons the defense and retreats to Faramir's bedside. Gandalf takes command of the city's defenders, moving tirelessly between the gates and walls to rally the flagging spirits of the men. The enemy launches a horrific barrage of flaming missiles and the severed heads of Gondor's fallen soldiers over the high stone walls.

Denethor commands his servants to build a funeral pyre in the Silent Street to burn himself and his son alive while the city falls. The great ram Grond, a hundred-foot beam tipped with a steel wolf's head, is brought forward by mountain-trolls to smash the Great Gate. The Witch-king of Angmar, riding a black horse and wielding a flaming sword, shatters the iron doors with spells of power and terror. Gandalf alone stands before the broken gate on Shadowfax, challenging the Lord of the Nazgûl as the first light of dawn approaches. The confrontation is interrupted by the sound of a cock crowing and the distant, thunderous blowing of the horns of Rohan in the North.




### 5: THE RIDE OF THE ROHIRRIM
The host of the Rohirrim is bivouacked in the Drúadan Forest, where they hear the rhythmic drumming of the Woses, the gnarled Wild Men. Ghân-buri-Ghân, a squat and mossy-bearded headman, offers to lead the king's army through a forgotten wain-road to bypass the enemy blockade. Théoden accepts the guide's help, promising that the Wild Men will no longer be hunted like beasts if the mission succeeds in saving Gondor. The army moves silently through the Stonewain Valley, a narrow pass carved into the hills by the ancient inhabitants of the mountain-land. Scouts find the bodies of Hirgon and his companion, indicating that the errand-riders were intercepted before they could reach the city.

As they reach the edge of the forest, Ghân-buri-Ghân senses a change in the wind and vanishes back into the trees with his folk. The king's host passes through the breaches in the outer wall of the Pelennor, which have been left unguarded by the overconfident orc-armies. Théoden gazes at the burning city of Minas Tirith and feels a moment of doubt before the morning wind from the sea touches his face. The king stands tall in his stirrups and blows a great blast on a horn that bursts asunder, signaling the charge of his six thousand Riders. Théoden leads the first éored on his white horse Snowmane, his golden shield shining like an image of the sun in the new light.

The Rohirrim burst into a terrible song of battle as they crash into the unsuspecting hosts of Mordor on the northern fields. The darkness is swept away by the rising dawn, revealing the white horse banner on a green field fluttering at the head of the charge. The orcs and Haradrim are trampled under the hoofs of the horses, and the siege-engines are set ablaze by the vengeful Riders of the Mark. Merry clings to Dernhelm as they gallop into the fray, witnessing the king outpace his own knights in a fey and unstoppable fury. The sound of the horns and the singing reaches the walls of the city, bringing hope to the survivors of the long and desperate night.
